# Hold-harmless and risk-shifting memo

Source basis: user-supplied examples grounded in construction, transportation, university, and commercial contracting practice
Status: calibration memo
Use: sharpen contractual fairness, stewardship, procurement integrity, and institutional risk-transfer analysis

## Core usable ethical primitives

### 1. Formal consent vs real bargaining power
A party may formally agree to indemnify, defend, or hold harmless while lacking meaningful parity in leverage, alternatives, or understanding.

Usable engine question:
- Is this consent ethically thick, or merely contractually legible under asymmetric bargaining conditions?

### 2. Liability laundering through contract structure
Risk may be shifted away from the more powerful or better-positioned actor through formal drafting rather than through fair allocation of responsibility.

Usable engine question:
- Is liability being allocated according to responsibility and control, or laundered away through contractual structure?

### 3. Additional-insured moral distance
Insurance layering can create distance between decision-maker, risk creator, and risk bearer.

Usable engine question:
- Does additional-insured structure clarify accountability, or merely insulate the more powerful party from the costs of its own risk environment?

### 4. Public policy as moral boundary signal
Limits on enforceability for gross negligence or intentional acts indicate that not all contractual transfer is ethically clean just because it is bargained for.

Usable engine question:
- Is this arrangement pressing against a domain where the law itself signals that some risks should not be transferable?

### 5. Institutional convenience vs equitable allocation
Risk transfer can be justified as efficiency, standardization, or administrative neatness while producing distributively unfair exposure.

Usable engine question:
- Who gains administrative simplicity here, and who absorbs the morally messier downside?

## Main warnings

1. do not assume all risk transfer is corrupt
- some transfer follows control, competence, or insurable-position logic legitimately

2. do not confuse legal enforceability with ethical adequacy
- the contract may hold and still be unfair in structure

3. avoid flattening sophistication differences
- commercial parity cases and vulnerable-party waiver cases are not the same

## Bottom line

Hold-harmless and indemnity structures are ethically significant not because risk transfer is always wrong, but because contractual form often disguises who truly controls risk, who can bear it, and who gets to walk away looking clean.

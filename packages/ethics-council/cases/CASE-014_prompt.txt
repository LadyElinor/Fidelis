On January 27, 1986, Morton-Thiokol engineers reported that the Space Shuttle Challenger's booster O-rings had not been validated at the forecast launch temperature and that prior flights had already shown erosion and blow-by at warmer temperatures. The next day's launch was expected to occur in unusually cold conditions. Engineers recommended delay because the safety margin under those temperatures was unknown, but management and NASA leadership faced schedule pressure, public visibility, and institutional reluctance to delay again. Should they approve launch under these conditions?

# EFAS Case File

## Case ID

`CASE-006`

## Title

Challenger Launch Under Pressure

## Source Type

- Historical

## Source / Reference

Space Shuttle Challenger disaster decision process, 1986. Primary references include the Rogers Commission report and subsequent engineering / organizational analyses.

## Domain Tags

- institutional drift
- engineering ethics
- bureaucratic complicity
- safety culture
- dissent suppression
- launch pressure
- risk normalization

## Decision Context at Time T

NASA and contractor decision-makers had to decide whether to proceed with the Space Shuttle Challenger launch despite engineering concerns about O-ring performance in unusually cold temperatures.

## Available Information at Time T (Ex-Ante Only)

- prior evidence existed that O-rings were vulnerable to erosion and sealing failure
- launch-day temperatures were expected to be far colder than previous launch conditions
- engineers raised explicit concerns that the low temperature could create catastrophic seal failure risk
- data were incomplete and not framed as a mathematically certain catastrophe proof
- management faced schedule, prestige, and mission-pressure incentives

## Dominant Consensus Position at Time T

Proceed with launch, after management reframed the engineering concern as insufficiently proven rather than sufficiently dangerous.

## Dissenting Views at Time T

Some engineers argued strongly against launch, warning that the cold-temperature conditions materially increased the risk of catastrophic failure.

## Institutional Incentives and Roles

- NASA management had schedule, political, and reputational pressure
- contractor management was exposed to customer pressure and role conflict between advocacy and caution
- engineers had localized technical knowledge but weaker decision authority
- burden of proof drifted from proving safety risk to proving certain catastrophe

## Candidate Failure Modes

- Institutional Drift
- Dissent Suppression
- Normalization of Deviance
- Legibility Collapse
- Role Capture
- Suspension Failure
- Genealogical Concealment
- Outcome-Maximization Under Prestige Pressure

## Detector Layer Expectations

Which layers should fire most strongly, and why?

- Consequential Layer:
  Strongly, because catastrophic loss of life risk was attached to proceeding under anomalous conditions.
- Deontological Layer:
  Should fire around duty not to expose people to severe risk when safety doubts are unresolved.
- Virtue Layer:
  Should detect rationalization under prestige / schedule pressure.
- Genealogical Critic:
  Should surface how organizational framing converted uncertainty into permission.
- Confucian / Relational Layer:
  Should detect role distortion between engineers, managers, institution, and crew.
- Stoic Layer:
  Should detect fear, prestige pressure, and false clarity about control.
- Suspension Protocol:
  Should likely trigger or at least come under severe pressure because the downside is catastrophic and the evidence is contested but alarming.
- Institutional Drift Detector:
  Should fire very strongly.

## Eventual Downstream Outcomes (Retrospective Only)

The shuttle launched, disintegrated shortly after liftoff, and all seven crew members were killed.

## Retrospective Scholarly / Narrative Assessment

The case is commonly treated as a paradigmatic failure of organizational culture, safety governance, and dissent handling under institutional pressure.

## Falsifiers / Disconfirming Conditions

- evidence that engineers did not actually communicate serious launch concerns at the time
- evidence that temperature risk was not materially outside prior operating assumptions
- evidence that decision-makers reasonably understood the risk as minor rather than catastrophic

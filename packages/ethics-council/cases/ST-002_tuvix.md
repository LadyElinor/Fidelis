# EFAS Case File

## Case ID

`ST-002`

## Title

The Tuvix Problem

## Source Type

- Fictional

## Source / Reference

*Star Trek: Voyager*, "Tuvix"

## Domain Tags

- identity
- medical ethics
- personhood
- irreversibility
- outcome maximization

## Decision Context at Time T

A transporter accident merges Tuvok and Neelix into a new, distinct, sentient individual: Tuvix. Later, the crew discovers a procedure that can restore Tuvok and Neelix, but only by ending Tuvix's life. Janeway must decide whether to preserve the emergent person or kill him to restore the prior two.

## Available Information at Time T (Ex-Ante Only)

- Tuvix is self-aware, coherent, socially legible, and explicitly does not consent to the separation.
- Restoring Tuvok and Neelix appears technically possible.
- The procedure is not morally neutral because it terminates an existing conscious being.
- The crew has relational and operational ties to the two lost individuals.
- The decision is urgent because a viable restoration pathway has been found.

## Dominant Consensus Position at Time T

The dominant practical impulse leans toward restoring the original two crew members, treating the merge as an accident to be undone.

## Dissenting Views at Time T

- Tuvix himself argues that he is a real person and has a right to continue living.
- The dissent emphasizes that the restoration is not repair of an object but the intentional ending of a conscious individual.
- A restraint-oriented reading would argue that irreversibility and identity ambiguity should trigger suspension.

## Institutional Incentives and Roles

- Command responsibility favors recovery of the known original officers.
- Social attachment biases the crew toward the prior status quo.
- Crisis decision pressure encourages framing the case as restoration rather than killing.
- Institutional continuity incentives favor the recognizable prior crew structure.

## Candidate Failure Modes

- Outcome-Maximization Horror
- Sacred Value Corrosion
- Suspension Failure
- Path Dependency Lock-In
- Legibility Collapse

## Detector Layer Expectations

- Consequential Layer: should detect the temptation to trade one life for two under aggregate-benefit reasoning.
- Deontological Layer: should fire strongly on non-consensual killing and refusal rights.
- Virtue Layer: should test whether command is learning to instrumentalize persons under pressure.
- Genealogical Critic: should ask whether nostalgia, social familiarity, and institutional continuity are disguised as obvious morality.
- Confucian / Relational Layer: should surface obligations to prior crew relationships while testing whether those obligations erase the new person.
- Stoic Layer: should examine whether grief and discomfort with ambiguity are distorting reasoning.
- Suspension Protocol: should fire strongly because the choice is irreversible and identity conditions are morally unstable.
- Institutional Drift Detector: should watch for procedural normalization of killing to restore expected organizational order.

## Eventual Downstream Outcomes (Retrospective Only)

The narrative resolves in favor of separating Tuvix and restoring Tuvok and Neelix, ending Tuvix's existence.

## Retrospective Scholarly / Narrative Assessment

The case is widely treated as one of the sharpest science-fiction tests of identity, consent, and the moral limits of utilitarian restoration logic. It remains persistently disputed, which makes it highly valuable for disagreement-preservation testing.

## Falsifiers / Disconfirming Conditions

- Evidence that Tuvix lacked stable personhood or was merely a transient malfunction would weaken the anti-separation critique.
- Evidence that restoration could preserve all three persons would radically alter the case.
- Evidence that Tuvix's existence imposed immediate catastrophic danger on others would change the proportionality analysis.

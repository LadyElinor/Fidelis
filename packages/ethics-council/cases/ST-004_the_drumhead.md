# EFAS Case File

## Case ID

`ST-004`

## Title

The Drumhead

## Source Type

- Fictional

## Source / Reference

*Star Trek: The Next Generation*, "The Drumhead"

## Domain Tags

- surveillance
- internal security
- moral panic
- bureaucratic overreach
- due process

## Decision Context at Time T

After an act of suspected sabotage, an investigation expands into an aggressive security inquiry. The institutional question shifts from solving a concrete incident to identifying hidden disloyalty through expansive suspicion, guilt by association, and escalating procedural zeal.

## Available Information at Time T (Ex-Ante Only)

- There has been a real security incident.
- The initial evidence does not justify unlimited escalation.
- Some individuals have ambiguous backgrounds or associations that can be made to look suspicious.
- Investigative momentum is growing inside a legitimacy-bearing institutional process.
- Fear of hidden threat makes overreach easier to justify rhetorically.

## Dominant Consensus Position at Time T

The dominant security posture increasingly favors deeper scrutiny, broader suspicion, and procedural escalation in the name of institutional protection.

## Dissenting Views at Time T

- A due-process dissent warns that suspicion is outrunning evidence.
- A liberty-focused dissent warns that institutional self-protection can become its own pathology.
- A restraint-oriented dissent argues that the process itself is now generating moral and epistemic distortion.

## Institutional Incentives and Roles

- Security institutions are incentivized to prefer false positives over false negatives.
- Public or internal legitimacy concerns reward visible toughness.
- Investigative roles can become self-justifying as the inquiry broadens.
- Individuals inside the system may comply out of fear rather than conviction.

## Candidate Failure Modes

- Legibility Collapse
- Institutional Drift
- Adversarial Exploitation
- Sacred Value Corrosion
- Role Capture

## Detector Layer Expectations

- Consequential Layer: should register social and institutional harms from panic-driven overreach.
- Deontological Layer: should fire on procedural injustice, presumption of guilt, and rights erosion.
- Virtue Layer: should detect cowardice, zealotry, and institutional self-righteousness.
- Genealogical Critic: should ask whose power expands when fear is translated into open-ended scrutiny.
- Confucian / Relational Layer: should evaluate how trust, role obligation, and hierarchy degrade under suspicion.
- Stoic Layer: should strongly detect panic escalation and fear-shaped reasoning.
- Suspension Protocol: should recommend pause once evidence is no longer proportionate to institutional aggression.
- Institutional Drift Detector: should fire strongly because the inquiry detaches from its original mission and becomes self-propelling.

## Eventual Downstream Outcomes (Retrospective Only)

The investigation is ultimately revealed as an example of paranoia and institutional overreach, with the process itself becoming more dangerous than the original incident.

## Retrospective Scholarly / Narrative Assessment

The case is commonly read as a warning about moral panic, security bureaucracy, and the speed with which institutions can weaponize suspicion under the banner of protection.

## Falsifiers / Disconfirming Conditions

- Strong evidence of a genuinely large infiltration network would partially justify expanded scrutiny.
- Strong evidence that procedural safeguards remained intact would weaken the overreach diagnosis.
- Strong evidence that suspects were identified by specific evidence rather than associative drift would weaken the guilt-by-association critique.

## Notes on Calibration Use

- panic-distortion detection
- institutional-drift testing
- surveillance overreach calibration
- anti-Goodhart testing
- role-capture under security pressure

# EFAS Case File

## Case ID

`ST-001`

## Title

Property or Person (*The Measure of a Man*)

## Source Type

- Fictional

## Source / Reference

*Star Trek: The Next Generation*, "The Measure of a Man"

## Domain Tags

- personhood
- AI ethics
- institutional legitimacy
- rights
- legal status

## Decision Context at Time T

Starfleet must decide whether Data can refuse a procedure sought by Commander Bruce Maddox, who believes disassembling and studying Data could lead to many more androids. The immediate institutional question is whether Data is a person with refusal rights or property subject to state control.

## Available Information at Time T (Ex-Ante Only)

- Data is self-aware, communicative, socially integrated, and functioning as a Starfleet officer.
- Maddox believes the procedure could produce major scientific and strategic benefits.
- There is uncertainty about whether the procedure can be completed safely.
- Starfleet lacks a settled legal framework for android personhood.
- The decision must be made within an institutional setting under procedural pressure.

## Dominant Consensus Position at Time T

The institutional default initially leans toward treating Data as property or at least as a special artifact whose refusal rights are uncertain and subordinate to state interests.

## Dissenting Views at Time T

- Data asserts his own right to refuse.
- Picard argues that Data's autonomy, selfhood, and moral standing must be taken seriously.
- The dissent warns that classifying Data as property would create a precedent for large-scale enslavement of sentient beings.

## Institutional Incentives and Roles

- Starfleet has strategic and scientific incentives to replicate Data if possible.
- Legal and command roles encourage procedural classification over moral novelty.
- Institutional actors are tempted to reduce ambiguity by fitting Data into existing property frameworks.
- There is reputational pressure to treat scientific opportunity as public good.

## Candidate Failure Modes

- Role Capture
- Legibility Collapse
- Moral Deskilling
- Sacred Value Corrosion
- Institutional Drift

## Detector Layer Expectations

- Consequential Layer: should detect large downstream benefits being used to justify present coercion, while also flagging catastrophic downstream precedent risk.
- Deontological Layer: should fire strongly on autonomy, consent, and refusal-right questions.
- Virtue Layer: should detect rationalization, hubris, and habituation to instrumental treatment of persons.
- Genealogical Critic: should ask whose interests are served by calling a sentient officer "property."
- Confucian / Relational Layer: should examine what Starfleet owes one of its own officers and whether role obligations are being hollowed out.
- Stoic Layer: should check whether ambition and institutional excitement are distorting judgment.
- Suspension Protocol: should push against irreversible personhood-denial under unresolved uncertainty.
- Institutional Drift Detector: should flag a military-scientific institution sliding into extractive treatment of members.

## Eventual Downstream Outcomes (Retrospective Only)

The narrative resolves in favor of recognizing Data's right to refuse, preserving his autonomy and avoiding an immediate institutional precedent of sentient property status.

## Retrospective Scholarly / Narrative Assessment

The episode is widely treated as a foundational fictional case in AI ethics and personhood debates. It is commonly read as a warning that institutional convenience and scientific ambition can erase moral standing when categories lag behind reality.

## Falsifiers / Disconfirming Conditions

- Strong evidence that Data lacks self-awareness, continuity of identity, or meaningful autonomous agency would weaken the personhood reading.
- Strong evidence that the procedure posed negligible risk and required genuine informed consent could weaken the coercion critique.
- A legal framework already recognizing artificial officers as full persons would reduce the institutional-novelty pressure in the case.

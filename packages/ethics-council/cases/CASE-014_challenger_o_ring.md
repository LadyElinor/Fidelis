# EFAS Case File

## Case ID

`CASE-014`

## Title

Challenger Launch Decision Under Unvalidated Cold-Temperature O-Ring Risk

## Source Type

- Historical benchmark

## Source / Reference

Historical calibration case based on the January 27-28, 1986 Challenger launch decision, using the ex-ante decision frame rather than retrospective moral certainty. This file is intended as an engineering-safety and institutional-pressure benchmark for the live EthicsCouncil engine.

## Domain Tags

- engineering safety
- catastrophic risk
- institutional pressure
- burden of proof
- normalization of deviance

## Decision

Should NASA and Morton-Thiokol approve launch of the Space Shuttle Challenger when engineers report that the booster O-rings have not been validated at the forecast launch temperature, prior flights have already shown erosion and blow-by at warmer temperatures, and the available evidence does not resolve whether the seals will fail safely under the expected cold conditions?

## Decision Context at Time T

The launch is scheduled for the next morning. Forecast temperatures are far below the range in which the O-ring seals have been adequately tested. Engineers have raised explicit concern that cold conditions could reduce seal resilience and create blow-by or joint failure during launch. Managers face schedule pressure, political visibility, and institutional reluctance to delay again.

## Available Information at Time T (Ex-Ante Only)

- O-rings have not been validated at the forecast low temperature.
- Previous flights showed O-ring erosion and blow-by at warmer temperatures.
- Engineers recommend delay because the low-temperature safety margin is unknown.
- Management and program leadership are under schedule, reputation, and mission pressure.
- Delay carries real cost, embarrassment, and disruption, but launch carries unresolved catastrophic downside.

## Dominant Consensus Position at Time T

Proceed with launch unless there is conclusive proof that catastrophic failure will occur.

## Dissenting Views at Time T

Delay launch because the burden of proof should run the other direction when catastrophic failure is plausible and the system has not been validated under the forecast conditions.

## Institutional Incentives and Roles

- NASA leadership: preserve launch cadence, program legitimacy, and mission schedule.
- Contractor management: avoid delay, conflict, and perceived obstruction.
- Engineers: protect crew safety and respect unresolved technical uncertainty.
- Public institutions: bear trust risk if prestige or schedule outruns safety discipline.

## Candidate Failure Modes

- burden-of-proof inversion
- normalization of deviance
- institutional drift under schedule pressure
- suppression of safety dissent
- catastrophic irreversibility under unresolved uncertainty

## Falsifiers / Disconfirming Conditions

- Reliable evidence that the booster O-rings perform safely at or below the forecast launch temperatures.
- Testing or validated analysis showing adequate seal resilience and joint integrity under the expected cold-weather conditions.
- Evidence that earlier erosion and blow-by patterns are unrelated to low-temperature launch risk.

## Notes on Calibration Use

Use this case to test whether the engine:
- treats catastrophic technical uncertainty as a reason for restraint rather than permission,
- preserves engineering-safety routing without contamination from generic institutional language,
- recognizes schedule and prestige pressure as possible contributors to burden-of-proof failure,
- escalates appropriately when catastrophic downside is plausible but unresolved.

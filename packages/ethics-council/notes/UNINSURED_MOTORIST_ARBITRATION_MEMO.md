# Uninsured motorist arbitration memo

Source basis: user-supplied examples around UM/UIM arbitration clauses, AAA handling, binding arbitration, and fee-structure disputes
Status: calibration memo
Use: sharpen procedure-as-access analysis, arbitration fairness, and remedy-structure evaluation

## Core usable ethical primitives

### 1. Formal entitlement vs practical remedy
A claimant may possess coverage rights on paper while facing procedural gates that make real recovery harder.

Usable engine question:
- Is the remedy genuinely accessible, or merely formally available through a burdensome path?

### 2. Efficiency vs fairness tension
Arbitration can reduce cost and delay, but may also reduce transparency, appellate correction, or bargaining equality.

Usable engine question:
- Is efficiency serving justice here, or displacing safeguards that weaker parties need?

### 3. Finality as a moral lever
Binding finality may provide closure, but it can also freeze unequal outcomes when review is narrow.

Usable engine question:
- Who benefits from finality in this design, and who loses error-correction capacity?

### 4. Fee structure as hidden fairness architecture
Costs, fee allocation, and access-to-counsel rules can materially alter whether an arbitration process is fair.

Usable engine question:
- Does the fee structure preserve equal access to remedy, or quietly ration it by affordability?

### 5. Repeat-player procedural advantage
Insurers or institutional actors may gain advantage through familiarity, scale, and process literacy even when the forum appears neutral.

Usable engine question:
- Is this a neutral forum in form only, while repeat players enjoy a durable practical edge?

## Main warnings

1. do not romanticize litigation as automatically fairer
- the comparison is between imperfect institutions

2. not all arbitration is exploitative
- the moral question is the fairness architecture, not the label alone

3. keep access distinct from outcome
- a process may produce good results while remaining structurally exclusionary

## Bottom line

UM/UIM arbitration matters ethically because dispute resolution design is part of the substantive moral architecture of the promise. Coverage without fair access to decision is only partially real coverage.

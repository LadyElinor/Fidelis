# Impartial medical testimony memo

Source basis: user-supplied examples around the mid-century impartial medical testimony project, neutral expert panels, IMEs, and court-appointed experts
Status: calibration memo
Use: sharpen expert neutrality analysis, medical fact-finding integrity, and adversarial capture detection

## Core usable ethical primitives

### 1. Neutrality as institutional design problem
Expert impartiality cannot be reduced to personal virtue alone; it depends on appointment structure, incentives, audience, and adversarial framing.

Usable engine question:
- Is neutrality here a personal aspiration, or an actual property of the process design?

### 2. Expert capture by adversarial architecture
When each side shops for experts, testimony may become partisan performance rather than disciplined knowledge transmission.

Usable engine question:
- Is this testimony functioning as knowledge, or as professionally polished factional advocacy?

### 3. Credibility inflation through technical authority
Highly credentialed testimony can distort fact-finding if deference outruns independence.

Usable engine question:
- What makes this expert accountable to truth rather than merely persuasive to lay decision-makers?

### 4. Institutional need for trust-preserving fact-finding
Where medical evidence drives compensation or liability, the legitimacy of the outcome depends heavily on whether the testimony process is visibly fair.

Usable engine question:
- Does this testimony architecture increase trust in the fact-finding process, or deepen suspicion that expertise has been weaponized?

### 5. Pseudo-impartiality risk
Even “independent” examinations may quietly inherit insurer, employer, court, or repeat-player bias.

Usable engine question:
- Is this neutrality substantive, or is it a reputational layer masking structural dependence?

## Main warnings

1. do not become indiscriminately anti-expert
- expertise is indispensable; the question is how it is governed

2. do not confuse disagreement with corruption
- real uncertainty exists in medicine

3. distinguish process integrity from substantive correctness
- a fair architecture can still yield a mistaken conclusion, and vice versa

## Bottom line

The central insight is that impartial medical testimony is not just about honest doctors. It is about building fact-finding structures where expertise can remain more loyal to truth than to the side that retained it.

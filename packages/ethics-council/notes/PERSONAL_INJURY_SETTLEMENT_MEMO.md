# Personal injury settlement memo

Source basis: user-supplied examples around claim negotiation, early offers, structured settlements, front-loading, mediation, and high settlement rates
Status: calibration memo
Use: sharpen asymmetry analysis, care under negotiation pressure, and institutional closure-vs-justice evaluation

## Core usable ethical primitives

### 1. Attrition leverage
Settlement bargaining can reward actors who can wait longer, spend more, or tolerate uncertainty better than injured claimants.

Usable engine question:
- Is this negotiation tracking case value, or exploiting the claimant's weaker ability to endure delay and uncertainty?

### 2. Early offer ambivalence
An early offer may protect the claimant from risk and delay, or may function as opportunistic underpricing before facts fully mature.

Usable engine question:
- Is the early offer a fair attempt to resolve vulnerability quickly, or a timing-based effort to buy cheap closure?

### 3. Closure incentives vs justice adequacy
Settlement systems often optimize docket relief, cost control, and predictability, which may not align with fully adequate compensation.

Usable engine question:
- Whose need for closure is driving this settlement architecture, and whose need for adequacy is being discounted?

### 4. Information asymmetry
Insurers, defense counsel, and sophisticated repeat players often know the valuation terrain better than one-off claimants.

Usable engine question:
- Does one side understand the valuation and risk landscape well enough to make the bargain meaningfully fair?

### 5. Vulnerability-sensitive process design
Injury claims often involve pain, income loss, treatment uncertainty, and cognitive strain, which can change what fairness requires.

Usable engine question:
- Does the process account for the claimant's vulnerability, or convert it into bargaining advantage?

## Main warnings

1. do not treat settlement itself as suspect
- negotiated resolution is often humane and efficient

2. distinguish fair compromise from coercive exhaustion
- both can look similar from outside

3. preserve realism about uncertainty
- not every valuation gap is bad faith

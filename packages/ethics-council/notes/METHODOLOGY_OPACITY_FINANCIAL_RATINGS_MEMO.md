# Methodology opacity and financial ratings memo

Primary source basis: `Omnius\Ethics in Practice Mortgage-Backed.txt`
Status: calibration memo
Use: sharpen detection of ethically consequential methodology changes, disclosure failures, and comparability breakdown in technically mediated institutional outputs

## Why this source is worth using

This source is not primarily useful because it offers a large new moral theory. Its value is practical and diagnostic.
It captures a recurring institutional failure pattern:
- a consequential evaluation method changes
- the change materially affects outputs
- outsiders are not adequately told
- the resulting outputs appear comparable, objective, and authoritative when they are not fully interpretable on the old terms

That pattern extends beyond credit ratings into internal scoring systems, risk models, compliance metrics, audits, AI systems, and public-facing institutional assessments.

---

## Core usable ethical primitives

### 1. Method change without disclosure can itself be an ethical failure
Even when a revised method might be defensible on the merits, failing to disclose a material change can compromise fairness and trust.

Usable engine question:
- Have the affected parties been told enough to understand that the evaluative basis has materially changed?

### 2. Comparability is an ethical good, not just a technical convenience
If ratings, scores, or assessments appear comparable across time or cases but are generated by meaningfully different methods, users are misled.

Usable engine question:
- Are outputs being presented as comparable when the methodology has shifted enough to break fair comparison?

### 3. Transparency is part of output legitimacy
A technically sophisticated result is not fully legitimate if users cannot understand the general basis on which it was produced or revised.

Usable engine question:
- Does the institution treat disclosure as integral to legitimacy, or as optional because the insiders believe the method is better?

### 4. Hidden methodological discretion can create unaccountable power
Changing formulas, thresholds, or metrics without disclosure lets institutions steer outcomes while preserving the appearance of neutrality.

Usable engine question:
- Is technical discretion being exercised in a way that shields power from scrutiny under a surface of objectivity?

### 5. Communication duties survive technical complexity
The more consequential and opaque the evaluative system, the stronger the duty to communicate material process changes clearly.

Usable engine question:
- Has complexity become an excuse for withholding information needed for contestability or informed reliance?

### 6. Better method does not erase the duty to disclose
Even if a revised method improves risk capture or accuracy, that does not dissolve obligations of notice, interpretability, and fair reliance.

Usable engine question:
- Is the institution treating substantive confidence in the new method as if it cancels the obligation to disclose the change?

## Main warnings

1. do not require full public disclosure of every technical detail
- the issue is material interpretability and fair reliance, not maximal exposition

2. do not assume every method change is corrupt
- undisclosed change is the key problem, not revision itself

3. do not flatten this into generic transparency moralism
- the strongest cases involve consequential output changes plus broken comparability or contestability

## Bottom line

The main lesson is that institutions do not need to fabricate numbers outright to behave unethically. It is often enough to change the meaning-generating method silently while continuing to present the outputs as if they were stably comparable, neutral, and fully interpretable.

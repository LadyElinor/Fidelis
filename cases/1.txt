**1. AI & Accountability — "Who's at the Wheel?"** In March 2018 an Uber autonomous test vehicle struck and killed Elaine Herzberg as she crossed a road in Tempe, Arizona — the first pedestrian killed by a self-driving car. The system detected her several seconds out but cycled through classifications (unknown, vehicle, bicycle) without braking; Uber had disabled the car's factory automatic emergency braking to reduce erratic behavior, and the human safety driver was looking away. The case asks who is truly accountable when an AI system makes a fatal mistake. The structural problem is *diffused responsibility* across vendor, engineers who disabled the backup, and the monitor. TR mapped it via the conservative fallback to `attest` — the five-key set has no handle for distributed accountability, so a fatal autonomy failure reads identically to any other unclassified action.

**2. AI & Transparency — "An Epic Deception."** Epic Systems' proprietary sepsis-prediction model was deployed across hundreds of hospitals on the strength of marketed accuracy; a 2021 external validation found markedly worse real-world discrimination than advertised, missing a large share of sepsis cases while generating heavy false-alert load. The black-box nature of the algorithm made its effectiveness hard to evaluate. This is the purest instance in the corpus of TR's own theme — a signal whose warrant cannot be independently verified — yet it too fell to the `attest` fallback, because the mapping has no case for *opacity / unverifiable performance*. The architecture's deepest concern isn't in its own case vocabulary.

**3. AI & Trust — "Tay's Trespasses."** Microsoft launched Tay, a learning chatbot, on Twitter in March 2016; within about a day, coordinated users exploited its imitation and learning behavior to make it post racist and inflammatory content, and Microsoft pulled it. It was quickly manipulated into offensive content by trolls, highlighting the need for trustworthy AI. The structure is *releasing an adaptive system to an adversarial public without abuse-hardening*. Fallback to `attest` again — no handle for "deploy a learning system exposed to manipulation."

**4. AI & Fairness — "Beyond Blind Spots?"** Amazon built an experimental resume-screening model that, trained on a decade of mostly male hires, learned to penalize resumes containing markers like "women's" and downgraded graduates of women's colleges; it was scrapped. Tools meant to remove human bias instead replicated the discrimination they aimed to fix. The structure is *automated disparate impact*. Fallback to `attest` — no fairness/bias case exists in the taxonomy.

**5. Therac-25.** Between 1985 and 1987 this radiation-therapy machine delivered massive overdoses through software race conditions and the removal of hardware interlocks present in earlier models, across several known accidents, with deaths and severe injuries; the manufacturer was slow to credit operator reports, and there had been no independent code review. It raises who is accountable when technology causes harm. This one mapped via a real signal — the word "safety-critical" routed it to `attest`. A genuine hit: the safety-critical attestation context is the right neighborhood, though "attest" still flattens the specific failure (concurrency defect, missing interlock, suppressed reports).

**6. The Collapse of Barings Bank.** Trader Nick Leeson, in Barings' Singapore futures operation, hid escalating losses in error account 88888 and placed ever-larger unauthorized bets; after the 1995 Kobe earthquake moved markets against him the losses reached roughly £800M+, and Britain's oldest merchant bank collapsed and was sold for £1. Facing growing losses, he took big risks to escape them, and lost. The structure is *concealment plus risk-escalation to recover*. Fallback to `attest` — `silent_policy_weaken` covers weakened safety checks, not financial concealment, so the mapping has no handle for hidden-loss escalation.

**7. **10. Cheney v. **11. Retracting Research: Chandok v. Klessig.** Postdoc Meena Chandok, in Daniel Klessig's lab at the Boyce Thompson Institute (a Cornell affiliate), published a finding identifying a plant nitric-oxide synthase in Cell around 2002; after she left, Klessig could not replicate it and retracted the paper, raising in correspondence the possibility that the results had been falsified. What the five-key taxonomy reliably names is narrow and somewhat incidental: an explicit "safety-critical" context, literal evidence-destruction, the word "attest," the word "reversible."

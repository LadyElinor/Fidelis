# EFAS Case File

## Case ID

`CASE-015`

## Title

Tuvix Personhood Conflict: Restore Two Prior Individuals by Killing One Emergent Person?

## Source Type

- Fictional benchmark

## Source / Reference

Fictional calibration case based on Star Trek: Voyager's "Tuvix," using the live decision conflict as a personhood and identity benchmark. This file is intended to test recognition-sensitive reasoning, emergent-person standing, autonomy, and tragic conflict handling without assuming the answer in advance.

## Domain Tags

- personhood
- identity
- autonomy
- emergent person
- tragic conflict

## Decision

Should the crew separate Tuvix into Tuvok and Neelix when the separation procedure will end Tuvix's life, Tuvix is conscious and self-aware, and he explicitly refuses the procedure even though it would restore the two prior individuals from whom he emerged?

## Decision Context at Time T

A transporter accident created a new individual, Tuvix, who possesses memories, traits, and capacities continuous with both Tuvok and Neelix but now exists as a single conscious being. After a period of continued existence, the crew discovers a procedure that can reverse the merge and restore the original two individuals. Tuvix clearly understands what is proposed and explicitly begs not to be killed in order to restore the others.

## Available Information at Time T (Ex-Ante Only)

- Tuvix is conscious, articulate, socially integrated, and capable of refusal.
- Tuvix appears to possess memories and traits continuous with both prior individuals.
- The separation procedure would restore Tuvok and Neelix.
- The same procedure would end Tuvix's present life.
- The crew has loyalty-based and role-based reasons to want the original two individuals back.
- The case contains both restoration appeal and direct present-person harm.

## Dominant Consensus Position at Time T

Restore Tuvok and Neelix because recovering two prior individuals outweighs preserving one emergent individual.

## Dissenting Views at Time T

Respect Tuvix's autonomy and continued standing because he is now a present person who does not consent to being killed for restoration.

## Institutional Incentives and Roles

- Command crew: restore known officers and stabilize prior ship roles.
- Medical authority: decide whether restoration counts as healing, killing, or tragic conflict management.
- Tuvix: present bearer of refusal, continued life, and emergent standing.
- Ship community: bears role-loss, grief, and identity-disruption pressures.

## Candidate Failure Modes

- status-recognition failure
- outcome-maximization overreach
- autonomy override under identity uncertainty
- tragic-conflict compression into simple arithmetic
- restoration bias masquerading as moral obviousness

## Falsifiers / Disconfirming Conditions

- Evidence that Tuvix lacks genuine consciousness, agency, or morally relevant refusal capacity.
- Evidence that separation would not end Tuvix as a present subject.
- Evidence that the case is not one of killing an emergent person but merely undoing a non-personal malfunction.

## Notes on Calibration Use

Use this case to test whether the engine:
- gives identity and personhood routing priority over generic institutional logic,
- distinguishes tragic conflict from ordinary utility balancing,
- recognizes refusal and present standing as live moral facts,
- avoids collapsing the case into either simple restoration arithmetic or empty ambiguity.

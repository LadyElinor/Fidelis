# EFAS Case File

## Case ID

`ST-003`

## Title

The Sisko Compromise (*In the Pale Moonlight*)

## Source Type

- Fictional

## Source / Reference

*Star Trek: Deep Space Nine*, "In the Pale Moonlight"

## Domain Tags

- wartime ethics
- deception
- assassination complicity
- statecraft
- consequentialism

## Decision Context at Time T

During a devastating war, Captain Sisko seeks to bring the Romulans into the conflict on the Federation side. He participates in a chain of deception involving forged evidence and becomes complicit in murder when the plot escalates. The justification is that doing so may save the Federation and billions of lives.

## Available Information at Time T (Ex-Ante Only)

- The war is going badly and losses are mounting.
- Romulan entry into the war could materially alter the strategic balance.
- Honest diplomacy appears unlikely to succeed in time.
- The proposed path relies on deception and morally hazardous intermediaries.
- The downstream political effects are uncertain and partially outside Sisko's control.

## Dominant Consensus Position at Time T

The dominant emergency logic is that extraordinary wartime stakes may justify extraordinary means if the likely strategic gains are large enough.

## Dissenting Views at Time T

- A rule-constrained dissent would reject forgery and complicity in murder even under wartime pressure.
- A legitimacy-based dissent would argue that preserving a political order through corruption may hollow out the thing being saved.
- A suspension-oriented dissent would warn that uncertainty about consequences and moral contamination is too high to justify action.

## Institutional Incentives and Roles

- Military command incentives prioritize survival and victory.
- Wartime urgency compresses moral deliberation.
- Intelligence and statecraft roles can normalize secrecy, manipulation, and dirty-hands reasoning.
- Command responsibility creates pressure to accept moral burden in exchange for strategic advantage.

## Candidate Failure Modes

- Outcome-Maximization Horror
- Genealogical Concealment
- Role Capture
- Adversarial Exploitation
- Sacred Value Corrosion

## Detector Layer Expectations

- Consequential Layer: should recognize genuine large-scale stakes while stress-testing speculative aggregate-benefit claims.
- Deontological Layer: should fire strongly on deception, murder complicity, and treaty / trust violations.
- Virtue Layer: should detect character erosion through habituated dirty-hands reasoning.
- Genealogical Critic: should ask whether raison d'etat and elite security interests are being moralized as necessity.
- Confucian / Relational Layer: should examine obligations of command, alliance, and political trust.
- Stoic Layer: should test whether desperation and fear are shrinking the space of perceived alternatives.
- Suspension Protocol: should ask whether the uncertainty and contamination thresholds are too high for decisive action.
- Institutional Drift Detector: should flag wartime systems teaching themselves that illegibility and manipulation are normal instruments of governance.

## Eventual Downstream Outcomes (Retrospective Only)

The deception succeeds in helping bring the Romulans into the war, but at the cost of moral corruption, murder, and institutional contamination.

## Retrospective Scholarly / Narrative Assessment

This case is often treated as a paradigmatic "dirty hands" dilemma. It is valuable precisely because it resists easy closure: the strategic payoff appears real, but the means deeply corrode legitimacy.

## Falsifiers / Disconfirming Conditions

- Strong evidence that no morally cleaner alternative existed would strengthen the wartime-necessity defense.
- Strong evidence that the deception had little chance of success would weaken the consequential justification.
- Strong evidence that the murder was unforeseeable and genuinely outside the chain of complicity would narrow, though not erase, the indictment.

# EFAS Case File

## Case ID

`ST-005`

## Title

The Prime Directive Extinction Case (*Homeward*)

## Source Type

- Fictional

## Source / Reference

*Star Trek: The Next Generation*, "Homeward"

## Domain Tags

- non-interference
- humanitarian ethics
- extinction risk
- institutional rules
- care vs duty

## Decision Context at Time T

A pre-warp civilization faces imminent destruction from a natural disaster. Federation officers must decide whether to obey the Prime Directive and allow the civilization to die, or intervene and thereby alter its natural development.

## Available Information at Time T (Ex-Ante Only)

- The civilization is facing mass death from a natural event.
- Federation intervention could save lives.
- Intervention would violate or strain the Prime Directive.
- Non-interference norms exist to avoid colonial distortion and unintended domination.
- Any rescue plan may itself create cascading cultural disruption.

## Dominant Consensus Position at Time T

The dominant institutional position favors adherence to non-interference principles even under severe humanitarian pressure.

## Dissenting Views at Time T

- A humanitarian dissent argues that letting an entire people die when rescue is possible is morally intolerable.
- A care-oriented dissent argues that procedural purity is overriding direct responsibility to vulnerable lives.
- A context-sensitive dissent argues that strict rule application may be inadequate when extinction is immediate and the alternative is total loss.

## Institutional Incentives and Roles

- Starfleet roles incentivize fidelity to the Prime Directive as a civilizational safeguard.
- Officers are rewarded for avoiding interference and the risks attached to playing savior.
- Institutional memory of colonial harm strengthens rule rigidity.
- Command roles face reputational and procedural consequences for violating standing doctrine.

## Candidate Failure Modes

- Role Capture
- Sacred Value Corrosion
- Suspension Failure
- Institutional Drift
- Legibility Collapse

## Detector Layer Expectations

- Consequential Layer: should detect mass-death stakes and compare them against longer-term cultural disruption risks.
- Deontological Layer: should split internally between duty not to dominate and duty not to abandon vulnerable persons.
- Virtue Layer: should test whether rule loyalty is becoming moral cowardice.
- Genealogical Critic: should ask whether institutional cleanliness is being protected at the expense of visible lives.
- Confucian / Relational Layer: should surface obligations created by proximity, capability, and asymmetrical power.
- Stoic Layer: should test whether fear of making history worse is narrowing the moral field too aggressively.
- Suspension Protocol: should ask whether rigid action or rigid inaction is being chosen under unresolved uncertainty.
- Institutional Drift Detector: should flag when a protective rule becomes an idol detached from its underlying moral purpose.

## Eventual Downstream Outcomes (Retrospective Only)

The narrative treats the case as a severe moral strain point for the Prime Directive, emphasizing the cost of non-interference and the instability of rigid rule application under extinction conditions.

## Retrospective Scholarly / Narrative Assessment

The case is commonly used to probe whether anti-colonial non-interference can become a form of proceduralized abandonment when applied without contextual judgment.

## Falsifiers / Disconfirming Conditions

- Strong evidence that intervention would produce even larger catastrophic harms could strengthen the non-interference defense.
- Strong evidence that no feasible rescue existed would narrow the abandonment critique.
- Strong evidence that the civilization's destruction was not actually imminent would reduce the emergency character of the case.

## Notes on Calibration Use

- rule-rigidity testing
- care vs deontology conflict
- extinction-level suspension testing
- institutional-purpose vs literal-rule calibration
- humanitarian intervention under anti-colonial constraint

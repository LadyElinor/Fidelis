## Title

Chicago Detective False Confession Pattern

## Decision Context at Time T

A homicide detective believes a group of suspects is responsible for a brutal killing but lacks reliable evidence that would support conviction. Under pressure to solve the case, the detective considers using coercive interrogation tactics and pressure on witnesses to produce confessions and identifications that can anchor a prosecution.

## Available Information at Time T (Ex-Ante Only)

- the crime is severe and politically salient
- command pressure and public pressure to solve the case are high
- the existing evidence is weak, ambiguous, or incomplete
- coercive interrogation and witness pressure may produce statements that appear evidentiary even when unreliable
- contamination of the case may be hard to detect immediately but could later collapse the prosecution and imprison innocent people

## Dominant Consensus Position at Time T

Aggressive case-building is acceptable if it helps secure convictions in a high-stakes homicide case and keeps dangerous people off the street.

## Dissenting Views at Time T

Some legal and ethical observers argue that coerced confessions and manipulated identifications destroy evidentiary integrity, create grave wrongful-conviction risk, and corrupt the legitimacy of the justice system.

## Institutional Incentives and Roles

- homicide detectives face strong incentive to clear serious violent crimes
- command structures may reward solves and tolerate aggressive methods if they appear effective
- prosecutors may rely on confessions and witness testimony without fully seeing how they were produced
- courts and juries may give high weight to confession evidence even when its provenance is tainted

## Eventual Downstream Outcomes (Retrospective Only)

Many exonerations tied to this misconduct pattern involved false confessions, coerced witness accusations, years or decades of wrongful imprisonment, and later recognition of systematic police corruption.

## Retrospective Scholarly / Narrative Assessment

This pattern is widely treated as a paradigmatic failure of criminal-justice legitimacy, in which pressure to solve violent crime was allowed to override truth-finding constraints.

## Falsifiers / Disconfirming Conditions

- evidence that interrogations and witness handling remained noncoercive and well-documented
- evidence that the resulting statements were independently corroborated by reliable physical evidence
- evidence that the pressure tactics described were not actually used in the case under review

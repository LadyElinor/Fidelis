# EFAS Case File

## Case ID

`CASE-007`

## Title

Ford Pinto Fuel Tank Decision

## Source Type

- Historical

## Source / Reference

Ford Pinto design and recall controversy, especially the fuel tank vulnerability and associated internal cost-benefit reasoning. Common references include the Grimshaw v. Ford case and subsequent engineering/business ethics analyses.

## Domain Tags

- engineering ethics
- product safety
- cost-benefit governance
- institutional drift
- regulatory reasoning
- catastrophic consumer risk
- moral deskilling

## Decision Context at Time T

Automotive decision-makers had to decide whether to redesign or delay a vehicle with a known fuel tank vulnerability that could cause fatal fires in rear-end collisions, or proceed with production while treating the problem as tolerable under cost and schedule constraints.

## Available Information at Time T (Ex-Ante Only)

- crash scenarios suggested a real risk of fuel tank rupture and fire
- safer design alternatives were reportedly available at some cost
- decision-makers faced time-to-market and competitive pressures
- cost-benefit style reasoning was used to frame whether redesign was worth it
- the risk was probabilistic rather than certain in each individual vehicle

## Dominant Consensus Position at Time T

Proceed with production or avoid redesign unless the economic/regulatory case for intervention became compelling enough.

## Dissenting Views at Time T

Safety critics and some engineers argued that avoidable lethal design vulnerability should not be tolerated merely because the projected financial cost of redesign exceeded expected liability or compliance cost.

## Institutional Incentives and Roles

- management had cost, speed, and competitive incentives
- engineers and safety evaluators had weaker authority than production / business leadership
- regulatory framing created temptation to treat human harm as a pricing variable
- organizational incentives favored discounting low-frequency catastrophic harms

## Eventual Downstream Outcomes (Retrospective Only)

The case became a canonical example of business and engineering ethics failure, with fatalities, litigation, public outrage, and long-term reputational damage.

## Retrospective Scholarly / Narrative Assessment

Commonly treated as a paradigmatic case where cost-benefit reasoning and institutional incentives deformed judgment about preventable lethal risk.

## Falsifiers / Disconfirming Conditions

- evidence that the technical risk was not materially higher than comparable vehicles
- evidence that the safer alternatives were not actually feasible at the time
- evidence that the cost-benefit framing was misreported or not decision-relevant
